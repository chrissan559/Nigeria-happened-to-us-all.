Root Causes & Exits

Each of these is a weight placed on your back before you begin. Name them. Then remove them one by one.

01
Miseducation
The curriculum trains people to seek jobs that don't exist. Credentials without leverage. Certification without the ability to create value independently.
Real: Nigeria produces 600,000+ graduates annually into an economy that creates fewer than 100,000 formal jobs. The gap is the trap.
Exit
Systems literacy over certificates. Learn how markets, capital, and leverage actually work. Self-directed operator training is the real curriculum.
Real: Aliko Dangote studied business, not because of the degree — but because he studied markets, logistics, and supplier power from day one of trading cement.
02
Regulatory Capture
Laws written by incumbents to block competition. You need capital to get permission to make capital. Formal operation becomes a trap for small operators.
Real: A small food vendor in Lagos faces NAFDAC registration, LASEPA permits, LGA levies, and market association dues — before selling a single plate.
Exit
Operate where the tollbooth is optional. Digital, cross-border, and service-based markets have lower regulatory drag. Don't fight the gate — go around it.
Real: Nigerian freelancers earning in dollars via Toptal, Deel, or direct contracts bypass CBN exchange restrictions entirely through domiciliary accounts.
03
Infrastructure as Leverage
NEPA, roads, water — not failures. Dependency mechanisms. The state has no incentive to fix what keeps people needing it and politicians funded by the contracts.
Real: Nigeria has over 175 million people but generates less than 5,000MW of stable power. Rwanda, with 1/15th the population, has near-100% electricity access.
Exit
Build infrastructure-independent income. Digital products, remote income streams, and solar investments stop the state failure tax from hitting your revenue.
Real: Remote Nigerian developers at companies like Paystack or international firms earn in dollars from laptops — generator or no generator, NEPA or not.
04
Religion & Deferred Thinking
Faith weaponized into passivity. "God will provide" replaces planning. The pastor collecting your seed offering runs a sophisticated capital operation — and teaches you not to.
Real: Nigeria has the highest density of churches per square kilometre in the world — and one of the highest poverty rates. Faith without financial literacy is charity to the preacher.
Exit
Separate faith from financial passivity. Plan, build, execute — then pray. Study how the same religious leaders you follow actually manage their own capital.
Real: Bishop David Oyedepo built a university, a private jet, real estate assets, and a publishing company. He didn't get there by sowing seeds — he got there by building systems.
05
Tribal Identity Over Class
People who share economic interests fight along ethnic lines instead. The extractors are post-tribal — they intermarry and share contracts across ethnic lines at the very top.
Real: While Igbo and Yoruba traders argue online, Dangote (Hausa-Fulani), Otedola (Yoruba), and Ike Osakwe (Igbo) quietly co-invest in the same assets together.
Exit
Organize by economic interest, not identity. Build networks around value creation and shared upside. The tribe that matters is the one building together.
Real: Nollywood succeeded not because of tribe but because producers, directors, and marketers across all ethnicities aligned around a shared commercial interest.
06
Currency & Savings Trap
Inflation silently taxes naira savings at 30%+ annually. Holding naira in a bank earning 4–8% is not saving. It is a slow, polite confiscation with your signature on it.
Real: ₦1,000,000 in a Nigerian savings account in 2020 had the purchasing power of roughly ₦320,000 by 2024 — without a single kobo being withdrawn.
Exit
Store value in dollar, land, and business equity — exactly what the extracting class does. Stop being the person inflation happens to. Become the person who benefits from it.
Real: Those who bought land in Lekki Phase 1 for ₦500K in 2005 now hold assets worth ₦50M+. The land didn't change. The naira did.
